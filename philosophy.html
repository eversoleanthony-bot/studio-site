Teaching Philosophy

The Presence to Own a Scene

Progressing as a performer takes more than a strong voice. It takes dramatic skill, a real sense of style, enough understanding of human nature to make a character believable, and the presence to own a scene, the kind you can't fake. Here's how I build that with my students, one lesson at a time.

With any new student, I spend our first lesson talking about vulnerability and shame resilience. Singing and acting ask you to do something genuinely hard: shed the walls you've built and let yourself be seen in public. That takes courage, and it's a process, not a switch you flip. My job is to build a space where you can lower your guard gradually, feel safe trying something new, and fail without flinching. I want you comfortable sitting in public solitude, saying your negative self-talk out loud, and leaning into fear instead of around it. Everyone makes fear-based choices every day, and so does every character you'll ever play. Understanding that in yourself, and being willing to look imperfect while you work through it, matters more than almost anything else in this training.

Vocally, I cross-train toward one flexible, powerful instrument that works across genres, rather than a different technique for every style. In recent years my teaching has leaned more heavily into motor learning, drawing especially on the work of W. Stephen Smith and Matt Edwards: isolate a single physical coordination, drill it, then unite it back into the whole. It's unglamorous, daily work, but it's reliable. Students who put in the coordination work see the gains, every time.

Repertoire selection isn't neutral for me either. I choose songs and roles that push a student's growth vocally, musically, and socially, leaning toward contemporary song and opera because that's where the social and psychological themes run deepest, the kind that stretch understanding and break down a few assumptions along the way. I want every student to find themselves somewhere in what they're singing.

I spend real time on tactical actioning: assigning an active, transitive verb to every beat and tactic shift in a song or scene, rather than defaulting to playing a general emotion. Most young performers arrive with a habit of playing the feeling directly, which reads as false to an audience and boxes them into one choice. Naming the action behind the emotion opens up options and makes the performance land harder. I also draw on Michael Chekhov's psychological gesture technique to get students out of stock, meaningless movement and into gestures that come from how the body actually carries an emotional state. Emotion isn't the goal there. It's the consequence of the action.

Physically, I train toward economy of gesture and eye focus. I never want to stifle a real impulse, but it's common for a young singer to flail their arms or invent nervous movement because their hands feel like they need to be doing something. Stillness is a skill I teach directly: it focuses an audience and clarifies subtext, and without deliberate choices about movement and where your eyes land, all the work underneath on tactical actioning can get hidden. Economy of gesture and eye focus is the polish that makes a performance read as finished rather than just prepared.

For students working in genres that are typically amplified, I build microphone technique into lessons from the start rather than saving it for the handful of chances most performers get during a tech week. That means real time with a handheld mic, a headset, and a studio condenser mic, not just one format, so you walk into an amplified performance already comfortable with what the technology changes about your sound and your choices. That comfort is what lets you take real risks once you're standing in front of an audience.

None of this works without the first thing I mentioned: a willingness to risk being seen, imperfections and all. The cross-training, the tactical actioning, the stillness, all of it is built on top of that willingness. It's where every lesson starts, and it's usually still what we're working on by the last one.
