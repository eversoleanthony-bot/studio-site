Teaching Philosophy

On Teaching the Singing Actor

My work with performers splits two ways: nurturing a flexible voice toward optimal coordination, and forging in each performer an interpretive instinct, an understanding of the human condition, and an awareness of what contributes to powerful stage presence. Here's how I do both, across private lessons, masterclasses, clinics, and productions I direct.

With any new student or group of students, I spend our first session together talking about vulnerability and shame resilience. Singing and acting ask you to shed the walls you've built and let yourself be seen in public. That's a courageous thing to ask, one people can't do all at once.

My job is to build a space where an artist can feel safe taking bold risks, get used to making character choices, and succeed at failing. I want them able to exist uncomfortably in public solitude, developing shame resilience, and leaning into fear. Every person, and thereby every stage character, makes fear-based choices every day. Sitting with one's own fear-based choices, and being willing to risk imperfection while working through them, is a practice, revisited and rebuilt with every role.

I believe in cross-training head and chest dominant voice production, putting students on an intentional trajectory toward a flexible, well-coordinated instrument that can excel in any genre or style.

My teaching leans heavily into motor learning and exercise physiology. I isolate one physical coordination at a time, train it until it holds up under different conditions, then unite it back into the full instrument. This is athletic training aimed at coordination: ask the voice for a little more than it's used to, always gradually, and keep training that gain once it's earned, or it fades. It's unglamorous, daily work, but it's incredibly reliable: a student who puts in coordination work will see real improvement, often faster than they expect.

I spend real time on tactical actioning: assigning an active, transitive verb to every beat change in a song or scene. Most young performers arrive with a habit of playing the general emotion directly. Emotion isn't something an actor can play. It can't be directed toward another person the way a verb can. Assigning an action shifts the work from attempting to feel something to truthfully pursuing something. Naming the action behind the emotion opens up options and makes the performance land harder. Often the emotion is the obstacle, the very thing a character has to fight through to reach what they want. When it doesn't show up strong enough, the answer is never to reach for more feeling. It's to raise what's actually at stake. Confront the obstacle honestly, and the emotion arrives on its own.

I also draw on Michael Chekhov's psychological gesture technique. Students fully physicalize a beat with their whole body first, then strip the movement away until only the impulse remains internally. Held in the mind that way, the gesture still shifts something physiological, visible even in complete stillness.

Small physical choices carry real weight onstage. I train toward economy of gesture and eye focus. I never want to stifle a real impulse, but it's common for a young singer to gesture without meaning, a nervous need to be doing something with their hands. Discomfort with stillness is usually what drives the habit. Restraint clarifies subtext, and without deliberate choices about movement and where the eyes land, the work underneath on tactical actioning gets hidden. Economy of gesture and eye focus is what makes a performance believable: a body reacting the way people actually move.

For non-classical students, I believe in helping my students adjust to the technology they will use in their careers. My musical theatre workshop courses consist of on-mic rehearsals from day one. The nuance and vocal adaptation needed to succeed with amplified performance require much more practice than most young performers get with just a few opportunities during tech week. When coaching genres that are traditionally amplified, I make sure students get comfortable with the conventions, technique, and dramatic possibilities that come with amplification. Time with the technology in rehearsal is what lets a performer take real risks on stage.

Ultimately, developing a complete performer is about cultivating the technical freedom and emotional courage to be entirely human on stage. By bridging the gap between vocal science, dramatic craft, and psychological safety, I empower artists to step past fear and embrace the power of vulnerability. When physical coordination meets clear tactical purpose, students have developed a system they can rely on. They become resilient, adaptable, and compelling storytellers who possess both the vocal skill to meet any artistic demand and the presence to truly connect with each other within a scene.
