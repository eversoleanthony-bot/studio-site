Psychological Themes of Zazà

Addiction, attachment, and the psychology of infidelity — the psychological architecture underlying Leoncavallo's opera and what it asks of the performers who inhabit it.

Leoncavallo's Zazà, adapted from the Pierre Berton/Charles Simon play that did very well at the Paris Vaudeville in 1898, is a work dense with psychological complexity. The opera examines the effects of poverty, single-parent households, deception, infidelity, principles of attraction, and the trauma response — alongside the particular varieties of sexism that pervaded fin-de-siècle Europe. Understanding this psychological architecture is not supplementary context for the performer — it is the engine of the drama.

Performances are scheduled for March 24–27 at SUNY Potsdam's Proscenium Theatre.

Intense, Passionate Romantic Love: An Addiction?

Early-stage romantic love shares many symptoms with substance, non-substance, and behavioral addiction — including euphoria, craving, and withdrawal. Research shows that romantic love activates the same dopamine-rich brain regions implicated in substance addiction, producing intrusive thoughts about the partner, idealization of the other person or the relationship, and a diminished capacity for critical evaluation.

For the performer playing Zazà, this is not a metaphor. The neurological reality of early-stage love explains the irrational intensity of her attachment to Milio — why she abandons her professional instincts, why she is incapable of reading the obvious signs of deception, why the discovery of his betrayal produces something closer to withdrawal than to ordinary heartbreak. She is not weak. She is, in the most literal sense, addicted.

Romance Addiction vs. Sex Addiction

It is worth distinguishing, for the purposes of character analysis, between romance addiction and sex addiction. Love and romance addicts are driven by the passion of a new relationship — by the neurological high of emotional intensity and idealization. Sex addicts are compulsively drawn to the neurological release of sexual activity itself, independent of emotional attachment.

Zazà is clearly a romance addict. Her attachment is to Milio specifically — to the idea of their relationship, to what he represents to her, to the future she has constructed around him. This distinction matters for the actor because it clarifies the stakes: what Zazà loses when she discovers the truth is not just a lover but the entire architecture of meaning she has built around him.

Affairs and the Impact of Discovery

The typical trauma response to the discovery of infidelity includes depression, anxiety, and PTSD-like symptoms — including obsessive revisitation of the affair's details, intrusive imagery, hypervigilance, and a shattered assumption of safety within the relationship. The betrayed partner often finds themselves unable to process the revision of history the affair requires: every memory must be re-evaluated, every tenderness re-examined for deception.

In the opera, the discovery of Milio's wife and daughter is not a gradual revelation but a sudden one. Zazà does not encounter abstract information — she sees the child. She hears her. This specificity is dramatically essential: it is not the concept of betrayal that breaks her but the concrete, embodied reality of what she is being asked to displace. The scene demands a performer who understands that Zazà's emotional collapse is not theatrical hysteria but a clinical trauma response.

Trauma Bonding

Zazà's history with childhood neglect and poverty creates the conditions for a particularly destructive pattern of attachment in adulthood. Trauma bonding — the formation of intense emotional attachment to an abusive or exploitative partner — is well-documented in individuals whose early caregiving relationships were marked by inconsistency, neglect, or loss. The pattern is not random: it is the predictable result of a nervous system calibrated to seek love in circumstances that replicate the conditions of its original wounding.

For Zazà, the abandonment of her ambitions and professional autonomy in favor of a relationship with a man who is systematically deceiving her is not naive — it is the expression of a deeply familiar emotional logic. She has been here before, in one form or another. The work of the performer is to locate the specific shape that logic takes in Zazà's body, her voice, her choices — and to make it legible to an audience as something recognizable, not as pathology but as the perfectly comprehensible consequence of a life that taught her, early and thoroughly, what love costs.
