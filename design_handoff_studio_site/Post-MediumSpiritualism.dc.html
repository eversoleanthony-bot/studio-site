The Medium: A History of Spiritualism

The historical origins of American Spiritualism, and what Menotti's opera illuminates about grief, belief, and the need to communicate beyond death.

"Farewell, Dear Athol!" At 6:30 p.m. on July 25, 1897, Athol Estes Porter died of tuberculosis in the home of her mother and stepfather. Her death was a crushing blow to her family, especially her husband, William Sydney Porter, the man who would later become famous under the pen name "O. Henry." At the time, there were those who may have comforted the grieving family by telling them that Athol was not gone at all, and that she could still communicate. These people were known as Spiritualists.

Modern Spiritualism began in the 1840s in a small town in New York, and quickly grew to become one of the greatest, and most divisive, religious movements of the 19th century. Almost all historians point to the movement beginning in 1848 in Hydesville, New York, where sisters Maggie and Kate Fox began communicating with a "spirit" in their home through rappings and knockings. Soon, spiritual mediums could be found in almost every town.

Religious Rebellion

Many gravitated toward Spiritualism because, like traditional religions, it believed in the immortality of the soul. Unlike traditional religions, it did not call for blind faith, skeptics could see concrete "proof" of an afterlife by attending a séance. This individualistic outlook meant that Spiritualism was the sole religion of its time that saw women as equals. Female mediums used this platform to champion the ideas of women's suffrage, equal rights, and the abolition of slavery, claiming that these weren't their own ideas but rather messages from the "spirit realm."

This Republic of Suffering

With the onslaught of the Civil War and the growing lists of men who would never return home, more and more people turned to Spiritualist mediums, hoping for proof that their loved ones' immortal souls were at peace. By the end of the war, a reported 11 million people subscribed to Spiritualism and 35,000 were practicing mediums. Grieving mothers, fathers, sisters, and wives participated in séances and spirit communication, hoping to receive one last message from their dearly departed.

Scientific Spiritualism

In the mid-19th century, American religious beliefs were shaken by scientific discoveries. Spiritualism fit the need for a religion that could maintain faith while aligning with science, touted as the "scientific religion," asking participants to observe demonstrations produced under "test conditions" in the séance room. Mumler's most famous spirit photograph is of Mary Todd Lincoln, who had become heavily involved in Spiritualism after the deaths of her son and her husband.

Believers, Skeptics, and Hucksters

Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, the creator of Sherlock Holmes, was an avowed Spiritualist who traveled the world lecturing on the comfort grieving families could find in a séance parlor. But there were also many frauds looking to profit from the grief-stricken. On October 21, 1888, Maggie Fox confessed that she and her sister had faked the rappings and knockings by cracking their knuckles and the joints in their feet. The world-famous magician Harry Houdini made it his life's work to expose the perpetrators, ultimately declaring: "after twenty-five years of ardent research and endeavor I declare that nothing has established between the Spirits of the departed and those still in the flesh."
