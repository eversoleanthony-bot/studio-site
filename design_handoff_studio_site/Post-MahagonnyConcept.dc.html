Mahagonny Songspiel — A Concept

A production concept document for Kurt Weill and Bertolt Brecht's Mahagonny Songspiel: nihilism, hedonism, grief, and the Weimar Berlin that made all of it possible.

Themes

  • The failure of God, morality, and moral platitudes to encompass the larger and more complex aspects of the human experience
  • Nihilism and hedonism as important markers in the abandonment of meaning
  • Acceptance of self over restrictive societal conventions
  • The emergence of the outsider as the insider
  • The temporary nature and artificiality of the "golden age," as a metaphor for youth

Mahagonny

Mahagonny, Bertolt Brecht's dream city dystopia, was clearly influenced by Weimar Berlin. The work addresses a post-war financial crisis, an emerging sex trade, increasing embrace of outsiders, and a developing cabaret scene alongside new artistic movements that grew increasingly emotionally detached and nihilistic. The opera's six-scene structure moves from enthusiasm about the proposed utopia toward tragedy, ending as the plunderers are plundered and one of the girls tells the audience that there is no such place as Mahagonny.

Weimar Berlin, 1919–1933

The Weimar Republic is the unofficial name given to Germany in the interwar period. During this era, Berlin emerged as Europe's intellectual and creative center. Nicknamed the "Babylon of the 20s," Berlin's city center experienced youthful vitality and explosive sexual freedom. This was the decade of Brecht, Weill, the Isherwoods, and the Bauhaus movement, and this creative freedom became offensive to rising conservative and extreme right-wing movements.

Weill's Hybrid Sound-World

Weill was substantially influenced by the Jazz Age. Throughout his career, he connected classical concert music with popular dance hall styles. His marriage to popular singer Lotte Lenya and collaborations with Bertolt Brecht pushed this hybrid approach further into what became recognizably "Weill." Mahagonny initiated numerous Brecht/Weill collaborations, including the renowned Dreigroschenoper (The Three-Penny Opera). While The Three-Penny Opera contains widely recognized numbers, Mahagonny has its own popular standard in "Alabama Song," covered by The Doors and David Bowie, among others.

Visual Language: Holbein, Lang, and the Bauhaus

Weill scholar David Drew declared Mahagonny to be a Totentanz, a Dance of Death, connecting the production's visual language to Hans Holbein's grim Renaissance woodblock series of the same name. Fritz Lang's 1927 film Metropolis, the tale of a utopian city built upon forced labor, was released the same year as the opera, and its visual style and color palette proved immediately appropriate.

The design employs blacks, whites, and grays with other muted tones, combining with the concept of using séance photographs as the visual inspiration for The Medium. Contrasts with a single accent color: perhaps red. The goal is a palette that honors the nihilistic visual language of the era while allowing the production's moments of genuine feeling to register against it.
