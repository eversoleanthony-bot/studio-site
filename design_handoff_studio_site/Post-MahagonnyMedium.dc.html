Mahagonny & Medium: The Search for Meaning

Two operas, one directing season: how grief, belonging, and the courage to drop our armor connect Weill/Brecht and Menotti across seventy years.

The Need for Belonging and Connection

People are hard-wired to seek out belonging and connection. Coming out of a global experience that made connection impossible, I want to highlight that basic need in these two productions. Connection is the lure of Mahagonny, similar to the way that Weimar Berlin lured those from all over the world who were ostracized for the way they loved and connected with others. All the characters in Mahagonny are seeking something more, and we receive the message that Mahagonny has left our sextet seeking pleasure, connection, and parties.

Grief and Grieving

Both Mahagonny Songspiel and The Medium center on grief and the grieving process. There are so many grief stories in The Medium: how the Gobineaus and Mrs. Nolan process their grief by attending séances. In 1969, Elisabeth Kübler-Ross first published On Death and Dying, which identified the five stages of the dying process, later also attributed as the five stages of grief:

  • Denial
  • Anger
  • Bargaining
  • Depression
  • Acceptance

Years later, David Kessler, Kübler-Ross's collaborator on On Grief and Grieving, added a sixth step to the grieving process: finding meaning. According to Kessler, "there is no way around the pain, but finding meaning provides a cushion." It is my interest to shine a light on this grieving process and emphasize the necessity to witness one another's grief. Kessler also said: "grief must be witnessed."

The Numbing Cycle and Searching for Meaning

In Mahagonny Songspiel, meaning, specifically belief and faith in deity as a source of meaning, takes a back seat. We enter a world of nihilism and hedonism: the abandonment of meaning and the search for temporary pleasures. The Medium takes the opposite approach through its study of grief and fear. These characters are all searching for meaning in their own way.

Shame and vulnerability researcher Brené Brown posits that "you cannot selectively numb." If you numb the hard emotions, you are also numbing the good ones. When people numb, they become miserable, which leads to searching for meaning. A search for meaning requires vulnerability, and a fear of vulnerability is once again numbed, and the cycle starts again.

"There are only two emotions: love and fear. All positive emotions come from love, all negative emotions from fear... If we're in fear, we are not in a place of love. When we're in a place of love, we cannot be in a place of fear." — Elisabeth Kübler-Ross

Brené Brown reminds us that "we all feel afraid, but not all of us armor up... they stay in the fear and work through it from a place of an open mind, open heart, and curiosity." This is an important lesson to learn as performers. Staying in the fear and leaning into it, rather than numbing it and pulling back from your most creative, salient work, will change things for you, and will allow your audience to experience your public solitude as you invite them in.

It's my sincere invitation to this entire cast to drop your armor. It will be a process, but we'll approach it together, as one, and it will be worth it in both your artistic and personal lives.
