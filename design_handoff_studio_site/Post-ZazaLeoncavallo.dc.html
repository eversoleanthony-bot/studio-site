Leoncavallo, Zazà & The Giovane Scuola

A biographical and historical introduction to Ruggiero Leoncavallo: the man behind Pagliacci, the rivalry with Puccini, and the opera that closed his most productive decade.

Giuseppe Verdi and the Giovane Scuola

The world of Italian opera during the 19th century was dominated by Giuseppe Verdi. As Verdi aged, his operatic output became increasingly sparse. Naturally, other composers rose up to fill the void. The most prominently revered today is Giacomo Puccini, whose early success overshadowed his contemporaries during the final decade of the century. These composers all operated in Milan between 1880 and 1900 and were dubbed the giovane scuola, the "young school": Puccini, Alfredo Catalani, Alberto Franchetti, Pietro Mascagni, Francesco Cilea, Umberto Giordano, and Ruggiero Leoncavallo.

Leoncavallo's Early Life and Music

Leoncavallo was born in Naples in 1857. His father was a judge, the plot of Pagliacci is reported to be a true account of a case over which his father presided. Leoncavallo enrolled at the Naples Conservatory, studied piano and composition, and moved to Bologna, Cairo, and eventually Paris, where he accompanied singers in music halls. A collaborator introduced him to publisher Giulio Ricordi and encouraged him to Milan, where he would eventually rub elbows with Ricordi, his protégé Puccini, and other prominent members of the Milanese operatic elite.

"There was to be found to be hanging about the Casa Ricordi a plump young man, rather short of stature, with a quaff of black hair dangle over his forehead like a question mark."

The Sonzogno Concorso of 1889 & Pagliacci

Sonzogno held a one-act opera composition competition in 1889, won by Pietro Mascagni's Cavalleria Rusticana. Using Mascagni's hit as a template, Leoncavallo penned Pagliacci, based at least partially on a real-life case his father had overseen. It not only met but surpassed the popularity of Cavalleria Rusticana, and the Cav/Pag double bill was born, birthing the verismo movement that would dominate the standard operatic repertory worldwide for years to come.

The Two Bohèmes

Puccini bumped into Leoncavallo at a café in Milan in March of 1893. Both were, coincidentally, working on an opera adaptation of Bohème. Leoncavallo claimed he had offered Puccini the libretto the year before, which Puccini had turned down and publicly denied. In 1926, friends and neighbors of the late Puccini confirmed that he had indeed been offered and declined Leoncavallo's libretto. Only one of the two operas eventually became a cornerstone of today's operatic repertoire.

Zazà

Leoncavallo's reputation today is one of a "one-hit-wonder," but his fifth opera Zazà had great, albeit temporary, international success for over two decades beyond its premiere in 1900. It was staged at the Metropolitan Opera in New York City in 1920 for three consecutive seasons and was chosen as iconic soprano Geraldine Farrar's farewell performance in 1922. Zazà is based on the Pierre Berton/Charles Simon play of the same name, which did very well at the Paris Vaudeville in 1898.

Three versions of Zazà were published over the years: the 1900 Prima Edizione, the streamlined 1919 Ultima Edizione, and the 1947 Nuova Versione. Our production draws from sections of both the 1919 and 1947 versions, employing more of our talented singers in the Act I ensemble while still preserving the developing instruments of our young artists-in-training.
