The Visual Language of Zazà

Two pioneers of commercial art, Alphonse Mucha and J.C. Leyendecker, and how fin-de-siècle illustration shapes the visual world of this production.

For each show I direct, I look for visual and aesthetic influences to shape the world and concept of the production. For Zazà I settled on two influential pioneers of commercial art. A soft and passionate color scheme of pinks, reds, browns, and flesh tones, consistent throughout Mucha's work, inspired me as a symbol for Zazà: idealistic, passionate, and full of life, with a naivité that proves to be the cause of much suffering. Leyendecker's men, on the other hand, are all about status, and our lead tenor Milio Dufresne's web of lies is certainly partially based on maintaining a certain status within his community.

Alphonse Mucha

The name of Czech artist Alphonse Mucha (1860–1939) is synonymous with Le Style Mucha, a decorative art form that made the artist famous in Paris at the end of the nineteenth century. Today, Alphonse Mucha is recognized for the bridge he created between "high art" and commercialism. His chance at fame came through a rush job to create a graphic design for a theatre poster for the globally famous actress Sarah Bernhardt. She liked his work and signed a six-year contract, guaranteeing him success. His peak years 1893–1903 perfectly encapsulate the era.

J.C. Leyendecker

During his life, Leyendecker's paintings on magazine covers and in advertisements were widely recognized by admirers nationwide. He was the most popular illustrator of his day, published in the most popular magazines, yet he moved among commuters on trains, mixed in markets and shops, virtually unrecognized by his adoring public. J.C. Leyendecker was a gay man at a time when it was nearly impossible to live such a life openly. Only in his artwork can one find sex symbols and homoerotic images. Today Leyendecker is recognized by the LGBTQ+ community for his long-standing domestic partnership with his life-partner and favorite model, Charles Beach.

Fin-de-Siècle Theatre & Scenic Influences

Because a large part of Zazà takes place backstage at the Alcazar music hall, a social hall similar to a cabaret or burlesque theatre, I wanted to stay true to my theme of sourcing the opera's aesthetic from fin-de-siècle advertising art. These images informed the design of the production's publicity materials as well as the set dressing in the first act.
