Amélie: Guimard, Art Nouveau, and the Scenic Aesthetic

Hector Guimard's Paris, its cast-iron entrances, riveted ornamentation, and democratic beauty, as the visual foundation for staging Amélie.

Hector Guimard

Hector Guimard practiced what he preached. His architectural creations embrace each branch of the arts, from painting and sculpture to graphics and typography. Today he is regarded as one of the most individualistic artists of his era and an innovating founder of Art Nouveau with an instantly recognizable personal aesthetic.

Guimard is the best-known French Art Nouveau architect, in French circles, "Style Guimard" referred to Art Nouveau itself. His work uses plastic, abstracted vegetal and floral imagery in iron, glass, and carved stone, typically twisted into irregular, asymmetrical forms. His Paris Métro entrances combine the movement's embrace of nature with technological advances and modernization. Their sinuous forms stand out against typical streetscapes and have become worldwide icons for mass transit design.

Entrances for the Paris Métropolitain

Guimard used cast iron for entrance structures, honoring the industrial workers using the system. He created three standard entrance types: enclosed, tripod canopies, and open to sky, employing twisted, organic vegetal forms. Red bulbs illuminating the entrances pulsated upon train arrival. Guimard invented the typography for the yellow porcelain signs, now known as the Métropolitain typeface.

Métropolitain Interiors

In exploring metro station interiors featured in Amélie, beautiful die-cast ornamentation and structural criss-cross bracing appear as potential thematic visual devices. This is very Parisian and emphasizes the need for strength and stability in architecture and human connection. The criss-cross bracing metaphorically represents arms reaching out to support one another, connection and belonging as the central theme of Amélie.

The clocks at Gare de Lyon are beautiful accents and potential scenic landmarks, filling a thematic role with the recurring "how to tell time" musical motif.

Café des 2 Moulins & Montmartre

The café where Amélie works is a real establishment used during filming. The Café des 2 Moulins is located in Montmartre at 15, rue Lepic, at the junction of Rue Lepic and Rue Cauchois. Its name references two nearby historical windmills: the Moulin Rouge and the Moulin de la Galette.

Amélie lives and works in Montmartre, near the historic Sacré-Cœur. The idyllic cobblestone streets paired with bistro awnings and sidewalk cafés create the feeling of Jeunet's idealized Paris, and the visual language of the world we are building on stage.
