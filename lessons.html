Online Sessions

Technology for Online Sessions

A good remote session depends on good audio. A few things make a meaningful difference.

  • Wired headphones are strongly encouraged. Bluetooth codecs introduce latency that makes real-time vocal feedback unreliable.
  • An external condenser microphone is highly recommended (USB or XLR). Built-in laptop and tablet mics are discouraged — they compress and color the sound in ways that make accurate feedback difficult.
  • Ethernet over Wi-Fi when possible. Packet jitter on wireless connections can degrade the frequencies most important to pitch and tone feedback.
  • Zoom in Original Sound / High-Fidelity Music Mode with noise suppression, echo cancellation, and automatic gain control disabled.
  • A secondary device for accompaniment is highly encouraged. Running tracks from your lesson device causes sync issues and can cut your voice out when you begin singing.

FaceTime and other mobile conferencing apps work in a pinch, but Zoom with proper audio settings will give us a noticeably better session.

All students receive access to a shared resource library containing studio exercises, public domain repertoire, and your active working scores. Copyrighted contemporary repertoire may require a licensed copy of your own.

Between Sessions

The Work Between Lessons

Sessions are a starting point. The real integration happens between them — in the practice room, in rehearsal, on stage, and everywhere else. Practice doesn't always mean vocalizing. It looks like a lot of different things.

  • Daily vocal work. A short, consistent warmup and maintenance routine tailored to where you are technically.
  • Custom drills. Targeted exercises from our sessions designed to address your specific technical focus areas.
  • Learning and memorizing repertoire. Score study, text memorization, and getting material into the body.
  • Text and character work. Digging into who you're playing, what they want, and how that lives physically.
  • Listening. Cast recordings, recitals, live performance — active listening with intention is real practice.
  • Going to shows. Watching other performers work is one of the most underrated forms of development.
  • Record and transcribe your lessons. Logging sessions in a dedicated lesson journal is one of the highest-value habits you can build — nothing slips through the cracks.

Students are encouraged to text with questions, breakthroughs, and concerns between sessions — that's what I'm here for. And if you're performing, I want to know about it. Showing up and cheering students on is genuinely one of my favorite parts of this work.

Policy & Package FAQ

Does the studio operate in person, online, or both?

The studio operates on a hybrid model. In-person sessions are held at Day Murray Music in Salt Lake City; remote lessons are available worldwide via Zoom or FaceTime. Session format is confirmed at scheduling and can vary week to week based on student location and availability.

How do bundle credits work, and when do they expire?

Bundle credits are tracked and redeemed through the booking portal at the time of each booking. To maintain consistent vocal progress, bundles have a built-in shelf life (60 days for the 4-pack, 120 days for the 8-pack). Because studio space is limited, unused credits cannot be extended or transferred past their expiration date.

What are the technical requirements for remote lessons?

Getting the most out of a remote session depends on good audio. The following setup is strongly encouraged before your first online lesson.

  • Wired headphones are strongly encouraged. Bluetooth codecs introduce latency that makes real-time vocal feedback unreliable.
  • An external condenser microphone (USB or XLR) is highly recommended. Built-in laptop and tablet mics are discouraged — they compress and color the sound in ways that make accurate feedback difficult.
  • Ethernet over Wi-Fi when possible. Packet jitter on wireless connections can degrade the frequencies most important to pitch and tone feedback.
  • Zoom in Original Sound / High-Fidelity Music Mode with noise suppression, echo cancellation, and automatic gain control disabled.
  • A secondary device for accompaniment is highly encouraged. Running tracks from your lesson device causes sync issues and can cut your voice out when you begin singing.
Why is a separate device recommended for accompaniment tracks?

Running accompaniment tracks from your lesson device causes audio lag and interrupts your vocal signal when you begin singing — Zoom interprets the track as background noise and gates your mic. Running tracks from a separate phone or tablet keeps the audio clean and synchronized. A wired speaker gives the most reliable playback; a Bluetooth speaker works well in most rooms.

Can I alternate between in-person and online lessons?

Absolutely. The studio is built to be completely flexible. You can book an in-person session at Day Murray Music one week and switch to an optimized remote live-stream the next. All of your package credits redeem interchangeably when booking your next session.

How do we handle sheet music and digital scores?

We keep our workflow entirely digital and seamless. You will have access to a shared workspace containing a comprehensive working PDF archive of studio exercises, public domain materials, and active repertoire. To ensure strict copyright compliance, if we dive into copyrighted contemporary or specialty repertoire, you may occasionally need to purchase an authorized digital or physical copy for your personal use.

What happens if I need to reschedule a session?

Life happens, especially when navigating performance schedules and rehearsals. You can easily modify your lesson time via your booking confirmation up to 24 hours in advance without losing your credit. Because studio space is carefully allocated, cancellations or no-shows within that 24-hour window cannot be refunded or rescheduled.
